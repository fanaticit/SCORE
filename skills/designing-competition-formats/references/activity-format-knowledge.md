# Activity Format Knowledge

This reference stores reusable competition-format lessons learned. Update it as the project
learns more activities, rule variants, and organizer preferences.

## Chess

Default recommendation: Swiss.

Use Swiss when:

- there are too many players for a full round-robin
- players should keep playing throughout the event
- the organizer wants structured pairings and a fair overall ranking
- the event is in-person and time-limited

Offer these options:

- Swiss: best for balanced competition and full-event experience.
- Knockout: best for drama, match pressure, and clear brackets.
- Arena: best for convenience, volume, and quick online action.

Use knockout when:

- the organizer wants a simple bracket
- match pressure and elimination drama matter more than everyone continuing to play
- the schedule is tight and a clear winner matters

Use arena when:

- the event is online or hybrid
- flexible participation matters
- activity volume, streaks, and convenience matter more than traditional pairings

Important questions:

- individual or team chess
- time control and round duration
- number of rounds
- whether first-round pairings should be random, seeded, or organizer-reviewed
- tie-break rules
- whether byes are allowed
- whether scores are win/draw/loss or custom

## Volleyball

Scoring fact: volleyball has no draws in normal match results.

Default recommendation depends on team count and courts:

- Small team count and enough time: round-robin or pool play into a final.
- Larger team count or limited courts: Swiss-style qualifying into a final.
- Very tight schedule: knockout or short pools.

Ask:

- match format: one set, best of three, timed game, or points target
- win-by-two rule
- rally scoring target
- courts available
- whether finals use longer sets

Do not design league tables that rely on drawn volleyball matches.

## Football

Scoring fact: football can have draws in league, group, or Swiss-style stages.

Default recommendation:

- Group or league stage can use win/draw/loss points.
- Knockout rounds need extra time, golden goal, penalties, or another decisive tie-break.
- Short community events often work well with group/Squad-style qualifying into a final.

Ask:

- points for win and draw
- whether draws are allowed before finals
- penalty shootout rule for knockout/final
- pitch count
- match length and changeover
- goal difference, goals scored, head-to-head, or fair-play tie-breaks

## Basketball

Scoring fact: basketball normally needs a winner, especially in finals.

Default recommendation:

- Small groups: round-robin into final.
- Larger groups/time-limited event: Swiss-style qualifying into final.
- Very short event: knockout.

Ask:

- overtime or sudden-death rule
- court count
- match length and changeover
- points, head-to-head, score difference, or points scored tie-breaks

## Netball

Scoring fact: netball can allow drawn league games if the organizer wants, but finals should
usually have a tie-break.

Default recommendation:

- Small groups: round-robin into final.
- Larger groups/time-limited event: Swiss-style qualifying into final.

Ask:

- whether league-stage draws are allowed
- final tie-break rule
- court count
- match length and changeover
- points and goal-difference tie-breaks

## Generic Format Tradeoffs

Swiss:

- Good for fairness and continued participation.
- Good when entrants exceed feasible round-robin capacity.
- Requires pairing logic and standings.
- First round should support controlled randomness or seeding, not name-only pairings.
- Allow first-round reset/regenerate before scores are entered.
- Later rounds should pair from standings or ranking and avoid repeat pairings where
  practical.

Round-robin:

- Good for small groups and transparent fairness.
- Becomes too long as entrants grow.
- Can be added after a Swiss phase if time remains and the organizer wants entrants to have
  more games before an optional final.

Knockout:

- Good for drama and a clear bracket.
- Weak for full-event participation because eliminated entrants stop playing.

Arena:

- Good for online, high-volume, flexible participation.
- Less suitable for traditional in-person scheduling unless activity stations are fluid.

Hybrid qualifying into final:

- Good for community events that need everyone active and still want a clear winner.
- Requires a clear final-start deadline.
