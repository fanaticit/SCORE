# Tournament Format Guide

## Swiss

Use Swiss when there are too many entrants for a full round-robin and you want everyone to
keep playing. It is especially useful for chess and larger community events.

Strengths:

- Fairer overall ranking than a single-elimination bracket.
- Entrants stay active.
- Works with a fixed number of rounds.

Tradeoffs:

- Requires pairing logic.
- Standings and tie-breaks must be clear.

## Round Robin

Use round-robin for small groups where everyone can play everyone.

Strengths:

- Easy to understand.
- Fair for small groups.

Tradeoffs:

- Grows too large quickly as entrant count increases.

## Knockout

Use knockout when drama, simplicity, and a clear bracket matter more than full-event
participation.

Strengths:

- Simple public bracket.
- Every match feels decisive.

Tradeoffs:

- Eliminated entrants stop playing unless consolation fixtures are added.

## Arena

Use arena for online or flexible high-volume events where participants can play many games.

Strengths:

- Convenient and fast.
- Good for flexible attendance.

Tradeoffs:

- Less suitable for traditional in-person fixture scheduling.

## Hybrid Qualifying Into Final

Use qualifying rounds, pools, or Swiss-style standings to keep people active, then run a
final for the top entrants.

Strengths:

- Good for community events.
- Balances activity with a clear winner.

Tradeoffs:

- Requires a clear final cutoff time.
